Deep Customer Analysis: Micro-Deck
Executive Summary
Micro-Deck targets young adults and students who experience initiation friction—the cognitive paralysis that prevents them from starting meaningful goals. Through customer interviews and research, we’ve identified that the core problem is not motivation itself, but the inability to break large, overwhelming tasks into concrete, bite-sized first steps. Our revised solution focuses on helping people create the habit of breaking down large goals into actionable pieces, especially in moments when executive capacity is low.

1. Understanding Executive Function: Trait vs. State
   Our customer analysis is built on a critical insight: executive dysfunction exists on a spectrum, and our solution serves users across that entire range.
   Trait-Level Executive Dysfunction
   Some users have chronic executive-function differences—ADHD, diagnosed executive dysfunction, or long-term attention and planning challenges. For these users, tasks like initiating homework, organizing steps, or resisting distraction require sustained, high cognitive effort every single day.
   State-Based Executive Dysfunction
   Other users are neurotypical but experience temporary executive impairment driven by sleep deprivation, stress, burnout, or cognitive overload. Research shows that fatigue and sleep loss impair prefrontal-cortex-based executive functions—working memory, planning, inhibitory control, and decision-making—even in otherwise healthy adults. A college student pulling late nights during midterms may experience initiation paralysis that looks identical to chronic executive dysfunction, even though the underlying cause is different.

Design Implication
Micro-Deck is designed for moments when executive bandwidth is low, regardless of whether the cause is ADHD, burnout, perfectionism, or exhaustion. Our ultra-simple UI, 2-minute micro-steps, and single-card focus reduce cognitive load precisely when sleep, stress, or neurodivergence have weakened executive control. 2. Three Concrete Customers in Specific Moments
Rather than generic personas, we define our customers as real people in specific moments when they need Micro-Deck. Each represents a testable, nameable scenario we can validate through interviews.
Customer 1: Sarah, 19, BYU Freshman (Burned-Out Perfectionist)
The Moment:
Sunday night, 9:47 PM. Sarah sits on her bed, laptop open to Canvas showing a 15-page research paper due Friday. She’s been staring at the assignment for 23 minutes. Her roommate just left for a study group. She feels a knot in her stomach and opens Instagram “just for a second.”
State + Trait:
High perfectionist standards (trait) meet severe sleep deprivation (crunch week, averaging 5 hours/night). Her executive bandwidth is depleted. The paper feels impossibly large; she doesn’t know where to start.
What She Needs:
Not a reminder—she knows it’s due. Not tracking—there’s nothing to track yet. She needs help turning “write 15-page paper” into “open Google Doc and write one sentence about the introduction.” A single, concrete 2-minute action she can do right now in her current exhausted state.
Real-World Analog:
One interviewee described homework as feeling “so big and difficult” that “the mental energy to start is too much, but once you start you realize it’s not that bad.” Another said, “The process to get started takes a lot of mental energy, which detracts from how many tasks they can do and they get drained faster.”

Customer 2: Marcus, 22, Senior with ADHD
The Moment:
Tuesday morning, 7:15 AM. The alarm just went off. Marcus has been meaning to start running for three weeks. His running shoes are by the door. He picks up his phone to “check the weather,” and 40 minutes later he’s on Reddit and TikTok. Now it’s too late to run before class.
State + Trait:
Diagnosed ADHD (chronic executive dysfunction) plus decision fatigue from managing a heavy course load. His phone is both his essential tool and his greatest enemy—every time he opens it, the “digital casino” pulls him away from his goals.
What He Needs:
A way to surface “put on running shoes” as the first micro-step immediately, before competing apps hijack his attention. He needs the breakdown to happen automatically, with zero friction, and he needs the tool to help him stay off distracting apps during the 2-minute initiation window.
Real-World Analog:
One interviewee described always having “so many tasks to be done, never ending checklist, and then get distracted—will start one task, then get distracted partway through and start another task, and get distracted partway through that and start another task.” Another interviewee who uses habit apps said she “magically ends up on Instagram” whenever her phone prompts her to do something, emphasizing the need for a non-distracting, minimalist interface.
Customer 3: Emma, 20, Pre-Med Student (Crunch-Time, Exhausted High-Performer)
The Moment:
Thursday afternoon, 3:30 PM. Emma just finished rugby practice (familiar, easy to initiate). She's exhausted. She opens her planner and sees “study organic chemistry Chapter 7” (unfamiliar, cognitively demanding material). She closes the planner and tells herself, “I’ll do it tonight after dinner,” but she knows she probably won’t.
State + Trait:
High-performing in familiar domains like sports and routine coursework, but struggles with unfamiliar or difficult tasks, especially when tired. Post-workout fatigue (state executive dysfunction) makes the unfamiliar task feel impossible to start.
What She Needs:
Help breaking “study Chapter 7” into “open textbook to page 142 and read one paragraph”—something she can do right now in her current exhausted state, not “tonight when she has energy” (which never comes). She needs the tool to meet her where she is, not where she wishes she were.
Real-World Analog:
One interviewee said it’s “harder to break down unfamiliar tasks or goals” and that “familiarity” determines whether he can easily start. Another mentioned that initiating is easy “if it’s something he wants to do,” but “if it’s something required of him, yes” it’s hard—pointing to how cognitive load and unfamiliarity create friction. 3. Jobs, Pains, and Desired Outcomes (Synthesis Across Customers)
Core Jobs They’re Trying to Do
Turn meaningful but vague goals (keep a 4.0, read more, build a new habit, start homework) into consistent, repeatable actions.
Remember and return to goals over time, not just during bursts of motivation or deadline panic.
Maintain progress on goals despite fluctuating energy, sleep, and cognitive capacity.
Pains We Observed
Overwhelm and cognitive paralysis at large tasks. Users don’t know how to start or how long tasks will take. One interviewee said she procrastinates because tasks seem “so big and difficult” and “the mental energy to start is too much.”
High initiation friction, especially for unfamiliar tasks. Familiar goals (like training) are easy to start; unfamiliar ones (studying, reading) require breaking down, and users don’t know how.
Procrastination and task-switching loops. Users start one task, get distracted, start another, get distracted again—cycle repeats. Never finish anything.
Forgetting goals after 1-2 weeks. One interviewee said, “I usually forget what my goals are about a week or two in.” Alarms and reminder apps don’t help because they don’t address what to do or how to start.
Existing apps are too complex, too gamified, or too distracting. Users described feeling overwhelmed by too many features or getting pulled into Instagram/TikTok when they open their phones.
Desired Outcomes
Clear, concrete first steps that make starting feel easy, especially for unfamiliar or large goals.
Low-friction initiation. Ability to act right now rather than “saving for later” (which leads to forgetting).
Gentle, non-shaming feedback. Progress focus rather than streak pressure or guilt. One interviewee appreciated that our app was “black and white (less distracting than colors or growing a plant)” and suggested a feature to “revisit” stuck habits rather than punish inconsistency.
Minimal distraction. A tool that doesn’t turn into another digital time-sink.
Turning the very act of breaking down habits into a habit itself. 4. Environment, Existing Tools, and Why They Fail
The Environment Our Customers Live In
Heavy academic schedules, part-time jobs, extracurriculars.
Chronic sleep deprivation (especially during crunch weeks).
Constant phone access—both a productivity tool and a distraction vortex.
Social pressure to be high-achieving, plus internal perfectionism.
Tools They Already Use
Calendars and reminders: Google Calendar, Apple Reminders, alarms.
Habit-tracking apps: Done, HabitShare, Habitica.
Paper and pen: Bullet journals, planners, sticky notes.
Why These Tools Fail Them in the Moments That Matter
Tool
Why It Fails for Our Customers
Calendars
Remind you when to do something, but not what the first tiny step is. No help breaking down large tasks. One interviewee said calendar reminders didn’t work because she’d still procrastinate.
Habit trackers
Focus on tracking completion (streaks, stats) rather than helping you start. One user liked Done’s reminders but disliked that useful features (stats) were locked behind subscriptions. Another said HabitShare only marks tasks at the end of the day—no help with initiation.
Paper/pen
No automatic prompts or accountability. Easily forgotten. One interviewee said paper goals are “not as easily accessible” (left at home, not there in a moment of need).
Gamified apps
Too many features, overwhelming, distracting. One interviewee mentioned the “Duolingo effect”—apps with too much going on lead users to abandon them entirely. Colorful, busy interfaces drain executive bandwidth.

The Gap:
None of these tools coach the breakdown step at the moment of starting. They either assume you already know the micro-step (calendars, reminders) or focus on post-action tracking (habit apps). For users with low executive bandwidth—whether from ADHD, exhaustion, or overwhelm—the missing piece is “what is the 2-minute version I can do right now?” 5. How Customer Research Refined Our Solution
Initial Hypothesis
We started by framing Micro-Deck as a tool to “help people turn goals into habits” through simple reminders and a minimalist interface.
What Customer Research Revealed
Through several customer interviews, we discovered that the real bottleneck is not sustaining motivation over time—it’s initiating the first step. Across every conversation, the consistent pain point was:
Not knowing how to break large, vague goals into concrete actions.
Feeling paralyzed at the moment of starting because the task seems too big.
Having goals fade after 1-2 weeks not because of lack of desire, but because users forget or avoid the mental work of figuring out “what do I actually do next?”
One interviewee explicitly said she struggles “not just with goals but things like homework—where it’s hard to just start because it seems so big and difficult (feels like so much work) so you procrastinate because the mental energy to start is too much.”
Revised Solution Focus
Helping people create the habit of breaking down large tasks/goals into actionable, bite-sized pieces.
Micro-Deck is now explicitly designed to help users practice and repeat the breakdown behavior until it becomes automatic. For Sarah, the habit we’re building is “open Micro-Deck and let it help me turn ‘write paper’ into ‘write one sentence.’” For Marcus, it’s “choose a 2-minute micro-step before opening Instagram.” For Emma, it’s “break ‘study Chapter 7’ into ‘read one paragraph’ when I’m exhausted.”
Over time, the goal is for users to internalize this breakdown process and need the app less—but in moments of low executive bandwidth (fatigue, stress, ADHD, overwhelm), Micro-Deck remains a reliable scaffold.

7. Conclusion: Our Customer Is “Anyone Whose Executive Bandwidth Is Low”
   Our customers are not generically “people who like goals” or “students who procrastinate.” Our customer is:
   Anyone—neurodivergent or neurotypical, student or professional, burned-out or simply exhausted—who experiences initiation friction in moments when their executive capacity is depleted.
   Through customer research, we’ve identified three concrete archetypes (Sarah, Marcus, Emma) representing the intersection of trait and state executive dysfunction. Our revised solution—helping people create the habit of breaking down large goals into actionable, bite-sized pieces—targets the exact leverage point these customers need: the cognitive work of turning “write paper” or “start running” into a 2-minute action they can do right now, even when their executive function is at its lowest. Micro-Deck is designed for that moment. It is a scaffold for the breakdown habit, a tool for low-bandwidth states, and a bridge from paralysis to action.
