# UX Review Report — Mara, The Overwhelmed Starter

**Persona file:** aiDocs/userResearch/persona2.md
**Date:** 2026-04-04
**Simulated by:** AI agent

---

## Pre-Session Context

1. Recent task I couldn't start: I have a commission due in six days. A pet portrait — a dog named Biscuit. The client has been incredibly kind and patient and that somehow makes it worse. I have the reference photos. I have my tablet. I have opened the file six times and closed it six times.
2. What made it hard: Every time I open it, the blank canvas feels enormous. Like I have to somehow get from nothing to something finished in one movement. And then I think about whether the style I'm using is actually good enough, and then I think about whether they'll hate it, and then I close the file and watch someone else make art on YouTube.
3. What I usually do: Convince myself I'll feel more ready tomorrow. Text my friend Renata saying "I'm spiraling again" and she talks me down but doesn't actually help me open the file. Sometimes I write the task on paper so I feel like I've done something.

*Okay — keep that in mind. Now I open MicroDeck.*

## Screen-by-Screen Review

### Welcome Screen

**First impression:** Oh. It's... very white. Very still. Most apps I've downloaded from that folder on my phone jump at me — animations, confetti, a character welcoming me. This one just sits there. I don't know if that's good or bad yet. It feels different.

**Copy and tone:** "Start the thing you keep putting off." I laugh a little, nervously. Yes. That's me. "One card. Two minutes. That's it." I read it twice. I'm waiting for the catch. There's no catch visible.

**Emotional response:** Cautious hope. The stillness is calming. I'm used to apps that demand energy from me immediately. This one isn't demanding anything yet. That feels like a small mercy.

**Interactive affordances:** One button: [Let's begin →]. I hold over it for a second. Starting something — even opening an app — can feel like a commitment I'm not ready to make. The arrow on the button is actually reassuring. It's telling me this is a beginning, not an ending.

**Delights:** The absence of anything I have to set up first. No name field. No "what are your goals?" questionnaire. I've abandoned apps on that screen before.

---

### CreateCardGoalScreen

**First impression:** The keyboard comes up and I feel the familiar seize. The blank field is asking me something. "What feels big and difficult right now?" — I look at this for several seconds.

**Copy and tone:** This question is very honest. Almost too honest. "Big and difficult" — everything is big and difficult right now. The commission. The taxes I haven't done. The apartment that needs cleaning. The email from my mom I've been meaning to answer. I feel a small wave of that overwhelm, the one where everything rushes in at once and I can't pick anything.

**Emotional response:** For a moment I'm close to closing the app. Not because it did something wrong, but because it asked the right question at the wrong time, and I don't have a good answer. Then I think: Biscuit. The dog. Type the commission.

**Cognitive load:** High — not because the interface is complex, but because the question forces a real decision. For someone with decision paralysis, "what's the biggest problem?" is itself a hard problem. I'm grateful the placeholder gives me a concrete example — "Write my 15-page research paper" — it tells me I'm supposed to write one real thing, not everything.

**Friction points:** The blank field, combined with the open-ended question, is the highest-friction moment in the app for me. I don't abandon it, but I almost do. Someone with less determination on a worse day probably would.

**Missing elements:** I wish there was something slightly more scaffolded here — even a subtle example or a "having trouble? try typing the first stuck thing that comes to mind" prompt. The "I'm stuck" button lives on the *next* screen; a small parallel affordance here for users who can't even name a task would help.

### CreateCardActionScreen

**First impression:** My goal label is there at the top: "Start Biscuit commission." The context is preserved. That's good — I would have lost it otherwise.

**Copy and tone:** "What's one tiny step you could take first?" — "tiny" is the right word for me. I've been told to break tasks down before, but usually by therapists in a clinical way that doesn't stick. "Tiny" feels different. Less formal. More honest about the size of my dysfunction.

**I'm stuck — show ideas:** I tap this almost immediately. Not because I don't know the first step, but because I want to see if it'll tell me something useful. The consent dialog appears and I hesitate — "Sends text to OpenAI." I'm not sure how I feel about that. I toggle AI Suggestions on anyway, cautiously.

The chips appear: "Open the reference photo," "Set up a new layer," "Do a 30-second thumbnail sketch." I feel something shift. Whoever designed this wasn't writing generic productivity advice. They understood that I'm stuck on a *specific type* of task — a creative one — and the suggestions reflect that. I tap "Open the reference photo." That's actually the first step.

**Make this smaller:** I tap this too, after I've filled in the field. It doesn't replace my text; it offers a smaller version. "Look at the reference photo for 30 seconds." I choose this one. It's smaller. It's genuinely doable. It's the right step.

**Emotional response:** Something like relief. And mild surprise. I expected the AI feature to be performative — to make suggestions that sounded helpful but weren't. It wasn't performative.

**Delights:** "Make this smaller" is the most valuable feature in the app for me. The ability to shrink an already-small step into something that genuinely removes the barrier — this is the feature that might actually change things.

**Friction points:** The AI consent dialog adds a beat of hesitation I didn't expect. The language "Sends text to OpenAI" sounds more ominous than it probably is. I don't fully understand what that means for my data. A one-line reassurance ("Your task text is used to generate suggestions and not stored") would help.

### CreateCardConfirmScreen

**First impression:** My card. "Look at the reference photo for 30 seconds." It looks real. It looks like a thing I could actually do.

**Copy and tone:** "Ready for your tiny start?" — I feel something in response to the word "ready." I'm not sure I'm ready. But the subtext does something important: "You're just giving this 2 tiny minutes. You can stop after that or keep going." The explicit permission to stop is what gets me through the door.

**Emotional response:** Mild anxiety, held in check by the copy. The confirmation screen is designed to prevent the kind of last-minute retreat I usually make. And it works. I tap [Start now].

**Delights:** "You can stop after that or keep going" — this is the sentence that converts me. Without it, I'd tap [Save for later]. I do that with everything. But knowing I can stop after two minutes means I'm not committing to the whole commission. I'm committing to 30 seconds of looking at a photo. That's survivable.

### Timer Screen — Running

**First impression:** Full screen. My task. The countdown. The pulsing dot.

**Copy and tone:** Nothing to read. The task is there. The time is there. The dot is there.

**Emotional response:** I watch the dot. It's slow. It's gentle. I didn't expect it to be calming but it is — like something breathing alongside me. About 40 seconds in, I'm not thinking about the commission anymore. I'm thinking about what the reference photo looks like. I'm planning the composition in my head. I've started.

**Cognitive load:** The lowest it's been since I opened the app. There's nothing to decide. Back gesture is blocked — I notice this and feel oddly grateful. I can't leave.

**Friction points:** None mechanical. One moment of wanting to check Instagram (habit reflex) that passed because the screen left me nowhere to go.

**Delights:** The pulsing dot. I'll say it plainly — for me, in this moment, it was like having someone present with me without demanding anything. That's unusual for an app. I'm aware that sounds like a lot to ascribe to an animated circle, but it's true.

### Timer Screen — Complete + Explainer

**First impression:** "You hit your 2 minutes." I did. That's true. I usually don't.

**Copy and tone:** Not congratulatory. Not effusive. Just true. "You hit your 2 minutes." — I read it again. There's something in the matter-of-factness of it that doesn't embarrass me. If it said "You did it! 🎉" I would feel like I was being handled. This doesn't handle me.

**The Explainer Sheet:** "Two minutes was enough to start. The hardest part isn't the doing — it's deciding to begin. You just did that." I read this slowly. I've heard the second sentence many times, but never immediately after actually beginning something. Here, it's not advice; it's a report. It's telling me something that just happened. That changes how it feels.

**Emotional response:** I feel something I can only describe as small relief mixed with disbelief. Like: it worked? I actually started the commission (in my head, at least — I opened the reference photo mentally, which means I'll actually open it now). The app didn't praise me for being good. It just witnessed what happened.

**Delights:** "You hit your 2 minutes." — six words, no exclamation point. Perfect.

**Emotional response:** Mild warmth. The deck feels like a holding space, not a pressure list. One card. That's it. I don't feel the usual dread of looking at a task list.

**Friction points:** None in this state. A growing deck might trigger overwhelm — but right now, one card feels fine.

### Settings

**First impression:** Two toggles. I feel mild surprise and then relief. I was ready to find a streak counter somewhere. There isn't one. This is important.

**Copy and tone:** "Start each day with a blank deck. Yesterday's cards move to Past Days." — I read this carefully. My first reaction is mild alarm (where do they go?), immediately followed by relief when I see "Past Days" — they're not deleted. They're resting.

"AI Suggestions — Get ideas when you're stuck creating tasks. Sends text to OpenAI." I'm slightly uncomfortable with this. Not alarmed, just uncomfortable. I'd want to know more about what "sends text" actually means before I feel fully settled.

**Emotional response:** The absence of a streak tracker is a genuine relief. A streak counter would be the feature most likely to drive me away — the guilt of a broken streak is exactly the kind of shame spiral the app seems designed to prevent.

**Delights:** No streak. No badge. No "you've been on a 3-day streak!" No history visible. This is, for me, a feature. Someone designed this knowing that visible progress indicators are a liability for certain users.

### Just One Mode

I don't discover this on my own — I don't know what [⊟] means. I tap it by accident while trying to tap settings. When I land in Just One Mode, I feel brief confusion, then understanding.

**First impression:** One card, centered. [Start] and [Not today]. Oh. This is the version of the deck for bad days.

**Copy and tone:** "Not today" is exactly the right phrase. Not "Skip." Not "Later." "Not today" is a full sentence that makes a real decision feel acceptable.

**Emotional response:** Genuine warmth. I didn't know this screen existed and finding it accidentally felt like finding a hidden room that was built for me.

**Friction points:** The discoverability is low. I stumbled into it. Many users like me — who don't explore systematically — might never find it. The [⊟] icon is not self-describing.

**Missing elements:** A subtle tooltip or the first-time appearance of Just One Mode after the deck grows to, say, 4+ cards would help discoverability without adding complexity.

### Archive Prompt

I encounter this after swiping the commission card to defer it three times in my simulation of a two-week period.

**First impression:** "You've set this one aside a few times. Want to rest it for now?" — I read this and feel something release in my chest. I was expecting: "This task has been ignored for 7 days." I was expecting shame. I got "rest."

**Copy and tone:** "Rest it" is not "delete it." "Rest it" is not "you failed." "Rest it" is: this card is tired, maybe you're tired of it, that's okay. It'll be there if you want it.

**Emotional response:** Relief. Disproportionate relief, which is itself informative. The fact that I feel such intense relief from a "are you sure you want to archive this?" dialog tells me how loaded that question usually is in other apps. The word choice here is exceptional.

**Delights:** "Want to rest it for now?" — the "for now" makes it feel reversible. And it is reversible — archived cards are in Past Days. The copy accurately reflects the mechanics.

## Post-Session Questions

1. I opened a calm app. It asked me one hard question, which I almost didn't answer. Then it asked me a second question that was small enough to actually answer. Then I sat with a dot for two minutes and discovered I'd started thinking about the thing. Then the app told me that was enough. I didn't feel like I'd achieved something — I felt like I'd been un-stuck, which is different and better.

2. The first question — "What feels big and difficult right now?" — was the moment I was most confused, but confusion isn't quite the right word. Overwhelmed might be. I almost closed the app there.

3. Not pressured, no. Almost — when I was on the confirm screen, I wondered if tapping [Start now] was committing me to more than two minutes. The subtext answered that before I could fully panic.

4. When I tapped "I'm stuck – show ideas" — I expected a loading spinner for a long time, and then three unhelpful generic suggestions. The suggestions were actually specific and useful. That surprised me.

5. Completely different. A to-do list is a list of everything that's wrong. This app is for one thing. You do two minutes. Then it's done — the two minutes is done. You started. A to-do list has no ending, just accumulation.

6. If using it tomorrow didn't require remembering to open it. Notification at a specific time might help — not a badge, just a gentle ping. I worry I'll forget it exists the same way I forget everything.

7. "Make this smaller" surprised me. I didn't expect a button that would literally just... reduce the task. And then the result was genuinely smaller, not performatively smaller.

8. Settings. Not because it's bad, but because it's not the part I need. The two toggles are clean but I won't revisit them.

9. "It's for when you know what you should do but can't start. It gives you the tiniest possible version of the task and a timer, and then you just... do it for two minutes. That's actually all it is. It works."

## Overall Assessment

**Strongest moments for this persona:**
- "Make this smaller" on CreateCardActionScreen — this feature is Mara's lifeline; it transformed an abstract anxiety into a genuinely survivable two-minute task
- The Explainer Sheet — not advice, but a witness account; landed differently than expected
- Archive Prompt copy — "rest it for now" dissolved anticipated shame completely

**Biggest friction points:**
- CreateCardGoalScreen: the open-ended question with a blank field risks triggering decision paralysis before the app has built trust; there is no scaffold here for the moment when everything feels equally urgent
- AI consent language ("Sends text to OpenAI") creates a moment of low-level unease without a reassurance that data isn't stored; for an anxiety-prone user, vague data language is an unresolved concern

**Likelihood to return:** Medium-High
The first session worked and produced a real outcome. Return depends entirely on whether Mara remembers the app exists tomorrow — not a product flaw, but a discoverability gap that a single opt-in notification could solve.

**The one change that would most improve this persona's experience:**
Add a single supplemental line to the AI consent dialog: "Your text is sent to generate suggestions and not stored" — removing the low-level anxiety that "Sends text to OpenAI" creates without explanation, and maintaining the transparency that makes the copy good.
