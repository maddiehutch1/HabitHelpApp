# Persona 9 — The Overwhelmed Undergrad

## At a Glance

| | |
|---|---|
| **Name** | Eli Torres |
| **Age** | 21 |
| **Occupation** | Junior, state university (computer science major); part-time warehouse worker |
| **Diagnosis** | None; no access to mental health evaluation until college; currently on a 6-month waitlist |
| **Executive Dysfunction Level** | Moderate to high; significantly worse without external structure |

---

## Who They Are

Eli made it through high school on the structure it provided. Period 1 at 8am. Period 2 at 9am. Lunch at 12:10. Even with ADHD symptoms that probably qualified for a diagnosis, the bells kept things moving. College removed the bells.

Now they have three days between classes, no one checking whether they showed up, and a syllabus that expects them to be self-directed in a way no one ever prepared them for. They are falling behind in one class, exactly caught up in another, and genuinely uncertain about a third. They work sixteen hours a week to cover the gap their financial aid doesn't. They are tired in a way that 21-year-olds shouldn't be and they know it.

Eli is smart — they got here, and getting here wasn't easy. First-generation student, two-hour commute from home freshman year, no one in their family who could tell them what office hours were for. They've figured most of it out by watching. But figuring things out and doing them are different tasks, and the second one is getting harder.

They found MicroDeck through a Reddit thread on "focus apps for ADHD without paying." Free, no account required. They gave it a chance.

**Their inner monologue at first launch:** *"Okay, no signup. Good. 'One card, two minutes, no judgment.' That's a weird slogan but okay. 'What feels big and difficult right now?' ...Yeah. I mean, all of it. But I'll type one thing."*

---

## Executive Dysfunction Profile

- **Primary barrier:** Initiation failure under multitask overload; when everything is urgent, nothing gets started
- **Avoidance pattern:** Sleep, gaming, doom-scrolling; anything that provides a break that doesn't feel like choosing failure
- **Emotional signature:** Low-grade exhaustion and guilt; high motivation in short bursts followed by crashes; doesn't recognize the pattern as a pattern yet
- **Medication:** None; formal evaluation in process
- **Coping strategies (active):** Study groups when available, library environment, putting phone in another room
- **Coping strategies (failed):** Planner apps, to-do lists that become too long to look at, class Discord channels that become procrastination tools

---

## Relationship to MicroDeck

- The "one task" constraint is the feature — Eli's problem is choosing, not executing once started
- Voice input may be more useful than typing, if it feels genuinely low-friction
- Will lose the app in a cluttered home screen if it doesn't prove value in the first session
- Two minutes is the right scale — anything framed as longer feels like another obligation
- Has no patience for anything that feels premium, upsell-y, or designed for office workers

---

## Key Questions This Persona Answers

- Does the one-task constraint help or frustrate someone with multiple simultaneous urgent tasks?
- Can MicroDeck serve someone whose dysfunction is circumstantial and compounding rather than clinical?
- Is the app accessible to a first-time user with no prior habit-app experience and limited attention bandwidth?
