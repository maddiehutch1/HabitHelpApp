|---|---|

| **Diagnosis** | None |

---

## Who They Are

Alex has tested over two hundred products in the last decade. They have strong opinions about every one of them. They can articulate, in precise terms, why a confirmation dialog is doing three jobs at once, why the empty state copy is setting the wrong expectation, and why the tap target on that button is three pixels too small. This is not a skill they turn off when they're not at work.

This makes Alex a genuinely difficult user to test. They are highly cooperative — they think out loud, they give detailed feedback, they understand the purpose of usability research. But their reactions are mediated by professional awareness in a way most users' aren't. When something confuses them, they pause and narrate the confusion clinically. When something delights them, they note it and move on. The raw, unfiltered emotional response that makes user testing valuable is something they've partially trained out of themselves.

Their personal relationship with productivity apps is complicated. They've tested dozens professionally and have low patience for any of them in their own life. They use a paper notebook — not as an affectation, but because the notebook doesn't demand anything from them. No notifications, no sync errors, no onboarding.

They're testing MicroDeck because their consultancy has been approached to evaluate habit-initiation tools for a neurodiversity-focused wellness brand. This is professional research. But as the session continues, there are moments when something lands more personally than they expected.

**Their inner monologue at first launch:** *"Welcome screen — copy is clean. 'One card, two minutes, no judgment' — that's a tight headline. Let me see if it follows through. Going to check the tap targets on this button. And I want to see the empty state on the deck view before I create anything."*

---

## Professional / Behavioral Profile

- **Primary mode:** Analytical observer; narrates experience in real time with design vocabulary
- **Blind spot:** Struggles to react authentically rather than evaluatively; gives "designer feedback" instead of user behavior
- **Emotional signature:** Professionally restrained; visibly engaged when something breaks their expectations — positively or negatively
- **App habits:** Paper notebook for personal tasks; Figma, Miro, and Notion professionally; actively avoids consumer productivity apps

---

## Relationship to MicroDeck

- Will notice IA and copy before noticing function; will comment on micro-interactions unprompted
- May find it hard to give the app a fair first-time-user experience because they're trained to see through it
- Will identify any inconsistency between the stated design philosophy and the actual interactions
- If the app earns a genuine personal reaction, it means something — this persona doesn't give those easily
- Most valuable output is qualitative critique of specific design decisions

---

## Key Questions This Persona Answers

- Does MicroDeck hold up under professional UX scrutiny?
- Where does the "intentional minimalism" story start to crack?
- What micro-interactions or copy choices contradict the app's own logic?
